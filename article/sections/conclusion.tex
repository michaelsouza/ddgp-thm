\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We developed a rank-count theory for generic local solution sets in the Discretizable Distance Geometry Problem. The central point is that the obstruction in general DDGP counting is not the absence of branch structure; it is the loss of unlabeled global cancellation. Once we label violations by their mirror cliques, feasible local branch differences become the image of a kernel over $\mathbb{F}_2$.

This viewpoint explains why the classical clique-based no-pruning counts survive in DMDGP and Combinatorial DDGP: in those cases, the relevant cancellations are forced by graph structure. In nonconsecutive DDGP subproblems, the mirror labels identify exactly which cancellations remain valid locally. The rank formula therefore separates a local combinatorial component from the global weight dependence that prevents a general DDGP count.

Several questions remain open: how to select active subproblems automatically within a larger Branch-and-Prune computation; how to combine overlapping local counts while preserving the mirror labels that make cancellation valid; and how to characterize, detect, or exploit exceptional nongeneric cases where additional coincidences create more solutions than the generic rank count predicts.
