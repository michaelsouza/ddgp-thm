\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

By reformulating the combinatorial constraints of the Discretizable Distance Geometry Problem in the language of linear algebra over $\mathbb{F}_2$, this work establishes a rigorous bridge between local geometric reflections and global counting complexity. The core insight is that the complexity of DDGP counting under pruning is fundamentally governed not by a lack of branching structure, but by the algebraic interaction of labeled violations. When physical violations are systematically indexed by their corresponding mirror cliques, the space of feasible branch shifts emerges naturally as the projection $\mathcal{K}_F = M(\ker V)$. This linear structure determines the generic solution count under the stated mirror-separation assumptions, assigning an independent factor of two to each branch decision that escapes the algebraic reach of the pruning constraints.

This perspective provides a unified explanation for the tractability of classical DMDGP and unpruned DDGP instances, where graph-induced symmetries naturally guarantee full branch viability. In the presence of arbitrary pruning edges, the labeled violation matrix $V$ acts as a precise algebraic filter, identifying exactly which combinations of reflections preserve distance constraints. Crucially, the resulting rank formula cleanly decouples the purely combinatorial structure of the discretization graph from the measure-zero geometric coincidences that characterize degenerate physical embeddings.

Our algebraic framework opens a promising avenue for the design of highly efficient distance geometry algorithms. By shifting the focus from combinatorial search over exponential branch trees to linear operations over $\mathbb{F}_2$, this formulation provides the foundation for solver paradigms that bypass branch enumeration entirely. Specifically, future work will focus on exploiting the sparsity of the mask matrix $M$ and the labeled violation matrix $V$ to construct and rank the associated GF(2) systems efficiently for large-scale discretization networks. Integrating these algebraic filters into physical solvers holds the potential to significantly accelerate structure determination in molecular biology and sensor network localization.
