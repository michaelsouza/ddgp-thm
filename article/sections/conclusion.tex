\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We developed a rank-count theory for generic solution sets in the Combinatorial Discretizable Distance Geometry Problem. The central point is that the obstruction in general DDGP counting is not the absence of branch structure; it is the loss of unlabeled cancellation. Once we label violations by their mirror cliques, feasible branch differences become the image of a kernel over $\mathbb{F}_2$, with one independent factor of two for each branch decision not constrained by pruning.

This viewpoint explains why the classical clique-based no-pruning counts survive in DMDGP and Combinatorial DDGP: in those cases, the relevant cancellations are forced by graph structure. With pruning edges, the mirror labels identify exactly which cancellations remain valid. The rank formula therefore separates the combinatorial part of the count from the exceptional geometric coincidences excluded by generic mirror separation.

Several questions remain open: how to characterize the relation with the $K$-incident graph contraction conjecture; how to optimize the generator presentation for large instances; and how to detect or exploit exceptional nongeneric cases where additional coincidences create more solutions than the generic rank count predicts.
