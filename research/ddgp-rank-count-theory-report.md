# A Unified Rank-Count Theory for Local Solutions in the Discretizable Distance Geometry Problem

**Author**: Distance Geometry Research Group  
**Status**: Academic Preprint Seed  
**Date**: May 2026

## Abstract

We present a graph-combinatorial theory to count the generic number of local realizations in the Discretizable Distance Geometry Problem (DDGP) subject to a set of active constraints, including both base and pruning edges. Rather than relying on the exponential enumeration of the Branch-and-Prune (BP) tree, we formulate the counting problem as the dimension of an algebraic stabilizer space over $\mathbb{F}_2$. By constructing a family of graph-derived reflection generators (dependency cones and base-clique closures) and tracking their active-edge crossings via a canonical **labelled violation matrix** over a **labelled obstruction quotient space**, we prove that the local solution count is generically given by a simple rank formula:
$$
|\Xi_F| = 2^{\operatorname{rank} \begin{bmatrix} M \\ V_F \end{bmatrix} - \operatorname{rank}(V_F)}
$$
where $M$ encodes the generator masks and $V_F$ tracks the labelled edge violations. We present the algebraic formulation, proof architecture (using primitive bracket separation on a stretched moment-curve specialization), the counting algorithm, and empirical evidence in dimensions $K=2$ and $K=3$.

## 1. Introduction and Motivation

The Discretizable Distance Geometry Problem (DDGP) is a combinatorial subclass of the Distance Geometry Problem (DGP) in $\mathbb{R}^K$, where vertices can be ordered such that each non-seed vertex $v_i$ has a set of $K$ predecessors $U_i$ with known distances to $v_i$. In the counting convention used here, the first $K+1$ vertices are fixed as an initial rigid simplex, and binary decisions start at $v_{K+2}$. When the predecessor distances are exact and the predecessors form an affinely independent $(K-1)$-simplex, the search space for each non-seed vertex is restricted to at most two possible Euclidean coordinates. This allows the search for valid global realizations to be cast as a binary tree search, commonly solved via the **Branch-and-Prune (BP)** algorithm.

In the highly structured special case known as the **Discretizable Molecular Distance Geometry Problem (DMDGP)**, the predecessor set for each vertex is consecutive in the order:
$$
U_i = \{v_{i-K}, \dots, v_{i-1}\}
$$
Under this consecutive structure, the symmetry group of the unconstrained tree is generated by interval-based partial reflections. The **Symmetry-Based Build-up (SBBU)** algorithm exploits this consecutive structure to count and construct solutions by testing whether partial-reflection operators are compatible with pruning edges.

However, in the general DDGP, **predecessors do not need to be consecutive**. Predecessor sets $U_i$ can be any set of $K$ previously positioned vertices. This lack of consecutive structure breaks the linear interval-based symmetry group of the DMDGP:
- The geometric reflection hyperplanes (mirrors) no longer move in simple nested sequences.
- Reflections of early vertices propagate through complex dependency chains (directed acyclic graphs) rather than simple suffixes of the order.
- Traditional SBBU algorithms fail because they cannot define simple consecutive partial reflections.

To address this challenge, we develop a unified graph-combinatorial theory to count the generic number of local realizations associated with a set of pruning edges $P$ without enumerating the branches of the BP tree. We treat base edges and pruning edges uniformly as **active edge constraints**, replacing linear intervals with graph-derived generators.

## 2. Preliminaries and Notation

Let a DDGP instance be feasible in $\mathbb{R}^K$, with order $v_1, \dots, v_n$, and a fixed initial simplex:
$$
V_0 = \{v_1, \dots, v_{K+1}\}
$$
For each non-seed vertex $i > K+1$, let $U_i$ be its predecessor set of size $K$. 

### 2.1 Fixing Sets and Dependency Cones
Because predecessors are non-consecutive, the vertices needed to fix a later vertex and the vertices affected by an earlier binary decision are no longer simple intervals. Let the directed dependency DAG be defined by drawing an arc $u \to i$ if $u \in U_i$.

The **fixing set** of a vertex is its ancestral dependency set:
$$
\operatorname{Fix}_U(i)
=
\{i\}\cup\bigcup_{u\in U_i}\operatorname{Fix}_U(u),
$$
with $\operatorname{Fix}_U(i)=\{i\}$ for $v_i\in V_0$. This is the set of vertices whose positions must already be fixed in order to fix $v_i$.

The **dependency cone** of a decision vertex is the forward descendant set:
$$
\operatorname{Cone}_U(i) = \{i\} \cup \bigcup_{w: i \in U_w} \operatorname{Cone}_U(w)
$$
This represents the set of subsequent vertices whose coordinates are algebraically dependent on the branch choice at $v_i$.

### 2.2 Local Subproblems
For a set of pruning edges $P$, the local active subproblem is restricted to the vertices needed to evaluate the distances between the endpoints of $P$. Define the **local vertex set** $L_P$ as:
$$
L_P = \bigcup_{\{u,v\}\in P} \left(\operatorname{Fix}_U(u) \cup \operatorname{Fix}_U(v)\right)
$$
where $\operatorname{Fix}_U(u)$ is the ancestral set of $u$ in the dependency DAG, including $u$ itself. The **local binary decision set** is:
$$
B_P = L_P \setminus V_0
$$
which contains all decision variables (branch choices) that influence the local subproblem.

Let $F \subseteq E[L_P]$ be the **active edge set**. In pruning experiments, $F$ typically consists of the induced local base edges $E_0[L_P]$ and the pruning edges $P$:
$$
F = E_0[L_P] \cup P
$$

### 2.3 Local Solution Code
The **local solution code** $\Xi_F$ is the set of all binary branch strings in $\mathbb{F}_2^{B_P}$ that yield a valid coordinate assignment realizing every active edge in $F$:
$$
\Xi_F = \{s \in \mathbb{F}_2^{B_P} \mid s \text{ realizes every edge in } F\}
$$

## 3. Graph Generators

To characterize the symmetries of the local solution code, we define a family $\mathcal{G}$ of **graph generators** over $B_P$. Each generator $g \in \mathcal{G}$ is a pair:
$$
g = (m_g, C_g)
$$
where $m_g \in \mathbb{F}_2^{B_P}$ is a binary branch mask, and $C_g$ is a **mirror clique** representing the rigid coordinate hyperplane through which the mask reflects vertices. We define two types of generators:

### 3.1 Dependency-Cone Generators
For every decision vertex $q \in B_P$, the dependency-cone generator is:
$$
g_q = (m_q, U_q)
$$
where the support of the mask is the intersection of its dependency cone with the local decision set:
$$
S_q = \operatorname{supp}(m_q) = B_P \cap \operatorname{Cone}_U(q)
$$
The associated mirror clique is the predecessor set $U_q$.

### 3.2 Base-Clique Closure Generators
When multiple vertices share the same predecessor set (a common occurrence in non-consecutive lateration), they reflect through the same hyperplane. For every clique $C \subseteq L_P$ that serves as a predecessor set for some vertices, the base-clique closure generator is:
$$
g_C = (m_C, C)
$$
where the mask support is the union of cones of all vertices that share $C$ as their predecessor set:
$$
S_C = \operatorname{supp}(m_C) = B_P \cap \bigcup_{w: U_w = C} \operatorname{Cone}_U(w)
$$
These closure generators do not increase the linear span of the mask space, but they are essential because a group of vertices generated from the same mirror clique can act as a single geometric reflection block.

## 4. Active-Edge Labelled Violations

Let $g = (m_g, C_g)$ be a generator with support mask $S_g = \operatorname{supp}(m_g)$. 

### 4.1 Boundary Crossings and Violations
An active edge $e = \{a, b\} \in F$ is said to **cross the support boundary** of $g$ if exactly one endpoint lies in the support of the mask:
$$
|e \cap S_g| = 1
$$
If $e$ crosses the boundary, a coordinate reflection of the vertices in $S_g$ will move one endpoint while keeping the other fixed. The distance between them is preserved if and only if the fixed endpoint lies in the mirror clique $C_g$ (which lies entirely on the reflection hyperplane $H_{C_g}$). 

Thus, we say that $g$ **violates** an active edge $e$ if:
$$
|e \cap S_g| = 1 \quad \text{and} \quad e \setminus S_g \not\subseteq C_g
$$
This crossing constitutes a geometric obstruction: applying the mask $m_g$ will generically change the length of $e$.

### 4.2 The Labelled Violation Matrix
To track these obstructions algebraically, we label each violation not just by the edge, but by the **pair of the edge and the mirror clique**. This preserves the geometric type of the violation and prevents accidental cancellations between unrelated reflections.

The **labelled violation set** is:
$$
\mathcal{L}_F = \{(e, C_g) \mid g \in \mathcal{G}, \ e \in F, \ g \text{ violates } e\}
$$
For each generator $g \in \mathcal{G}$, we define its **labelled violation vector** $\nu_F(g) \in \mathbb{F}_2^{\mathcal{L}_F}$ as:
$$
\nu_F(g)_{(e,C)} = 1 \iff C = C_g \text{ and } g \text{ violates } e
$$
Let $A = \mathbb{F}_2^{\mathcal{G}}$ be the coefficient space of the graph generators. We define two linear maps by linear extension over $\mathbb{F}_2$:
- **Mask Map** $M: A \to \mathbb{F}_2^{B_P}$, which maps a generator combination $\alpha \in A$ to its combined branch mask $M\alpha = \bigoplus_{g \in \operatorname{supp}(\alpha)} m_g$.
- **Violation Map** $V_F: A \to \mathbb{F}_2^{\mathcal{L}_F}$, which maps $\alpha \in A$ to its combined labelled violation vector $V_F\alpha$.

The predicted stabilizer space of the local code is:
$$
\mathcal{K}_F = M(\ker V_F)
$$
This represents the space of all combined branch masks that can be presented with zero total labelled obstruction.

## 5. Presentation-Independent Obstruction

A branch difference $h \in \mathbb{F}_2^{B_P}$ can have multiple graph-generator presentations $\alpha \in A$ such that $M\alpha = h$. To ensure that the geometric obstruction attached to $h$ is well-defined and independent of the chosen presentation $\alpha$, we pass to a quotient space.

### 5.1 The Pure Presentation Space
Let $R_F$ be the space of **pure presentation labels** generated by combinations of generators that sum to the zero branch mask:
$$
R_F = V_F(\ker M) \subseteq \mathbb{F}_2^{\mathcal{L}_F}
$$
We define the **labelled obstruction quotient** as:
$$
Q_F = \mathbb{F}_2^{\mathcal{L}_F} / R_F
$$

### 5.2 The Canonical Obstruction Map
Since the dependency-cone masks are upper-triangular in the DDGP order, they span the entire branch space $\mathbb{F}_2^{B_P}$ (Lemma 4). Thus, for any branch difference $h$, there exists at least one presentation $\alpha \in A$ with $M\alpha = h$. 

We define the **canonical obstruction map** $\omega_F: \mathbb{F}_2^{B_P} \to Q_F$ as:
$$
\omega_F(h) = V_F\alpha + R_F
$$
This map is well-defined: if $M\alpha = M\beta$, then $\alpha+\beta \in \ker M$, so $V_F\alpha+V_F\beta \in R_F$, meaning they belong to the same coset.

### 5.3 Projected Kernel Identity
The projected kernel $\mathcal{K}_F$ is exactly the kernel of this canonical obstruction map:
$$
\mathcal{K}_F = \ker \omega_F
$$
*Proof.* If $h \in \mathcal{K}_F$, then $h = M\alpha$ for some $\alpha \in \ker V_F$, hence $\omega_F(h) = 0 + R_F$. Conversely, if $\omega_F(h) = 0$, choose $M\alpha = h$. Since $V_F\alpha \in R_F$, there exists a pure presentation $\delta \in \ker M$ such that $V_F\delta = V_F\alpha$. Then $M(\alpha+\delta) = h$ and $V_F(\alpha+\delta) = 0$, proving $h \in M(\ker V_F) = \mathcal{K}_F$. $\square$

The generic labelled-presentation theorem below uses this quotient structure to characterize active-edge preservation without reference to a particular presentation:
$$
h \text{ preserves all active edges generically} \iff \omega_F(h) = 0
$$

## 6. Main Theorems

We now state the core theorems of our rank-count theory.

### Theorem 1: Generic Solution Codes Are Algebraic-Stabilizer Translates
Assume $\Xi_F \neq \varnothing$. Define $\mathcal{U}_F(s_0)$ from a feasible base code $s_0 \in \Xi_F$ as the set of branch differences that preserve every active edge from $s_0$ as an algebraic identity.

Outside a proper algebraic exceptional set of coordinate parameters, for every $s_0 \in \Xi_F$:
$$
\Xi_F = s_0 \oplus \mathcal{U}_F(s_0)
$$
At this stage, $\mathcal{U}_F(s_0)$ is only a set of algebraic edge-preserving differences, so the statement is a translate rather than a vector-space coset. The labelled-presentation theorem below identifies it with the vector space $M(\ker V_F)$.

### Theorem 2: Generic Labelled Presentation (Lemma 6)
Assume $K\ge2$. For a generic DDGP realization with active edge set $F$, for every feasible base code $s_0 \in \Xi_F$ and branch difference $h$:
$$
h \in \mathcal{U}_F(s_0) \iff \omega_F(h) = 0
$$
Equivalently:
$$
\Xi_F = s_0 \oplus M(\ker V_F)
$$

### Theorem 3: Generic Labelled-Violation Rank Count
Assume $K \ge 2$, $\Xi_F \neq \varnothing$, and the DDGP instance is generic. Then, outside a proper algebraic exceptional set, the size of the local solution code is given by:
$$
|\Xi_F| = 2^{\operatorname{rank} \begin{bmatrix} M \\ V_F \end{bmatrix} - \operatorname{rank}(V_F)}
$$

## 7. Proof Architecture

The proof of the rank-count theory is structured in several layers, which we outline here.

### 7.1 Geometry of Individual Reflections
- **Lemma 1 (Lateration Choices Are Reflections)**: Let $C = U_i$ be the predecessor clique of a non-seed vertex $v_i$. Once $C$ is fixed, the two possible coordinates for $v_i$ are Euclidean reflections of each other through the affine mirror hyperplane $H_C = \operatorname{aff}(C)$.
- **Lemma 2 (Mirror-Compatible Edge Obstruction)**: Let $g = (m, C)$ and $S = \operatorname{supp}(m)$. Consider the ideal reflection reflecting $S$ through $H_C$ while keeping $L_P \setminus S$ fixed. If an active edge $e = \{a, b\}$ does not violate $g$ (i.e. is mirror-compatible), this reflection preserves the length of $e$ identically.
- **Lemma 3 (Single Violation Length Change)**: If $e$ violates $g$, this ideal reflection changes the length of $e$ generically. Setting coordinates such that $H_C = \{x_K = 0\}$, and $a \in S$, $b \notin S$, the change in squared distance is:
  $$
  \|a' - b\|^2 - \|a - b\|^2 = 4 h_a h_b
  $$
  where $h_a, h_b$ are the signed heights of $a, b$ over $H_C$. By nondegeneracy, $h_a \neq 0$, and generically $h_b \neq 0$ since $b \notin C$. Thus, the length changes outside the algebraic set $h_a h_b = 0$.

### 7.2 Spanning and Algebraic Covers
- **Lemma 4 (Cone Masks Span the Branch Space)**: The dependency-cone masks $\{m_q \mid q \in B_P\}$ form an upper-triangular matrix in the DDGP order, thus spanning $\mathbb{F}_2^{B_P}$.
- **Lemma 5 (Finite Exceptional-Set Lemma)**: For fixed $F$ and $s_0$, if $h$ does not preserve all active edges algebraically, the set of coordinate parameters where it accidentally preserves them is a proper algebraic subset. Because the number of branch strings is finite, the union of these accidental zero sets is still a proper algebraic subset.
- **Theorem 1 Proof**: Follows directly from Lemma 5 by excluding the finite union of proper algebraic accidental-zero sets.

### 7.3 Primitive Bracket Separation ($K \ge 2$)
The central mathematical challenge is completeness (Lemma 8): proving that a non-zero obstruction class $\omega_F(h) \neq 0$ cannot vanish algebraically due to accidental cancellations between different labels. We formalize this using oriented brackets.

For a $K$-clique $C = \{c_1, \dots, c_K\}$ and a vertex $z$, the oriented bracket is:
$$
[C,z]
=
\det
\begin{bmatrix}
\widehat{x}_{c_1}&\widehat{x}_{c_2}&\cdots&\widehat{x}_{c_K}&\widehat{x}_z
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
\widehat{x}_i=(1,x_i)^\top
$$
which is proportional to the signed height of $z$ over $H_C$. For an edge $e = \{a, b\}$, we define the primitive obstruction monomial as:
$$
\mu_{e,C} = [C, a][C, b]
$$
If $a \in C$ or $b \in C$, $\mu_{e,C} = 0$, representing mirror compatibility. Otherwise, it is generically non-zero.

- **Lemma 7 (Primitive Bracket Separation)**: Assume $K \ge 2$. In the coordinate ring of generic $K$-dimensional configurations, every non-zero obstruction class in $Q_F$ has a detecting primitive bracket combination:
  $$
  B_\varphi = \sum_{\varphi(e,C)=1} \mu_{e,C} \neq 0
  $$
  *Proof.* We specialize the generic coordinates to a stretched moment curve:
  $$
  x_i(t) = \left(t^{w_i}, t^{2w_i}, \dots, t^{Kw_i}\right)
  $$
  with increasing weights $w_i = N^i$, where $N$ is sufficiently large. Under this specialization, each bracket $[S]$ becomes a Vandermonde product with a leading exponent:
  $$
  E(S) = \sum_{p < q} \max(w_p, w_q)
  $$
  The primitive obstruction for a label $(e=\{a,b\}, C)$ has leading exponent $E(C \cup \{a\}) + E(C \cup \{b\})$. Because $w_i$ are powers of $N$, this exponent uniquely encodes the multiset of unordered vertex pairs. For $K \ge 2$, this pair-multiset uniquely determines the label $(e, C)$ (pairs appearing with multiplicity two are internal to $C$, and the endpoints are identified as vertices connected to $C$ by multiplicity-one pairs).
  
  Since distinct labels have distinct leading exponents under this specialization, any non-empty sum $B_\varphi$ has a unique largest exponent and cannot vanish identically. $\square$

- **Lemma 8 (Nonzero Labelled Obstruction gives Nonzero Edge Residual)**: If $\omega_F(h) \neq 0$, the coefficient of the primitive bracket monomial in the active-edge residual contains a factor of $4 h_a h_b \neq 0$. By Lemma 7, these monomials cannot cancel each other out in the quotient, so at least one active edge residual $\Delta_{e,s_0,h}$ is not identically zero.
- **Lemma 9 (Zero Labelled Obstruction Preserves Active Edges)**: If $\omega_F(h) = 0$, then $h \in M(\ker V_F)$. By grouping the generators by mirror clique, we apply block reflections one mirror clique at a time. Each step is mirror-compatible (Lemma 2) and preserves active edge lengths identically.

### 7.4 Rank-Nullity projected kernel dimension theorem
The linear-algebra identity for $\dim \mathcal{K}_F$ is unconditional. Define:
$$
T: A \to \mathbb{F}_2^{B_P} \oplus \mathbb{F}_2^{\mathcal{L}_F}, \quad T(\alpha) = (M\alpha, V_F\alpha)
$$
The image of $T$ is the subspace of combined masks and violation vectors. Let $\pi$ be the projection of $\operatorname{im} T$ onto the violation coordinates. Then $\operatorname{im} \pi = \operatorname{im} V_F$, so $\operatorname{rank} \pi = \operatorname{rank} V_F$. The kernel of $\pi$ inside $\operatorname{im} T$ is exactly:
$$
\ker \pi = \{(M\alpha, 0) \mid V_F\alpha = 0\} = \mathcal{K}_F \oplus \{0\}
$$
Applying the Rank-Nullity theorem to $\pi$:
$$
\dim \mathcal{K}_F = \dim \operatorname{im} T - \dim \operatorname{im} V_F = \operatorname{rank} \begin{bmatrix} M \\ V_F \end{bmatrix} - \operatorname{rank}(V_F) \quad \square
$$

## 8. The $\mathbb{F}_2$ Rank-Count Algorithm

The rank formula yields a highly efficient graph-combinatorial counting algorithm that operates purely over $\mathbb{F}_2$ and avoids exponential branch enumeration:

```text
Algorithm: DDGP-Labelled-Violation-Rank-Count
Input: 
  - order v_1, ..., v_n
  - predecessor sets U_i
  - base edges E_0
  - pruning edge set P
Output: 
  - predicted local valid count |Xi_P|

1. Compute ancestral discretization dependency sets Fix_U(i) for all vertices.
2. Build the local vertex set:
     L_P = union_{ {u,v} in P } ( Fix_U(u) union Fix_U(v) )
3. Build the local decision set:
     B_P = L_P \ {v_1, ..., v_{K+1}}
4. Formulate the local active edge set:
     F = E_0[L_P] union P
5. Construct the generator family G:
     - For each q in B_P, add cone generator g_q = (m_q, U_q)
     - For each distinct predecessor clique C, add closure generator g_C = (m_C, C)
6. Identify the labelled violation set L_F:
     L_F = { (e, C_g) | e in F, g in G,
             |e intersect supp(m_g)| = 1
             and the fixed endpoint is not in C_g }
7. Construct the binary matrices:
     - M of size |B_P| x |G|, where columns are mask vectors m_g
     - V_F of size |L_F| x |G|, where columns are violation vectors nu_F(g)
8. Compute rank over GF(2):
     rank_total = gf2_rank( [M; V_F] )
     rank_violation = gf2_rank( V_F )
     r_F = rank_total - rank_violation
9. Return 2^(r_F)
```

### 9.2 Non-Generic Geometry and Accidental Symmetries
In non-generic coordinate configurations, the actual number of local solutions can exceed $2^{r_F}$. This occurs due to **accidental mirror coincidences**:
- Collinearities or coplanarities in coordinates can cause an endpoint of a violating edge to lie accidentally on the reflection plane of a generator, even though the endpoint is not a member of the predecessor clique $C_g$.
- Under such circumstances, the algebraic length residual vanishes at that specific point, creating extra valid branches.
- Our theory rigorously identifies these as a proper algebraic exceptional set (zero set of the coefficient polynomials), which is Zariski closed and of measure zero in the parameter space.

## 10. Refinements Toward an Academic Article

To transition this research report into a journal-ready publication, two polishing tasks are identified:

### 10.1 The Degenerate $K=1$ Case
When $K=1$, the distance geometry problem reduces to positioning points on a line. 
- Hyperplanes are points, and Oriented Brackets become simple coordinate differences: $[S, z] = x_s - x_z$.
- The bracket separation proof (Lemma 7) must be adapted because the pair-multiset argument is degenerate for 1-cliques.
- A separate, simple linear algebra proof can be formulated for $K=1$, which should be appended to the paper for completeness.

### 10.2 Block-Reflection Normal Form
Lemma 9 uses block reflections grouped by mirror clique. For publication, this part should be expanded into a clean normal-form statement for the BP sign cover, since the order of Euclidean reflections can matter geometrically even when the induced branch masks are combined over $\mathbb F_2$.
- The normal form should make explicit why a zero-labelled presentation gives a valid algebraic motion on the iterated quadratic coordinate cover.
- This will strengthen the connection between the combinatorial matrix kernel and the field-theoretic stabilizer $\operatorname{Stab}_{\mathrm{BP}}(F)$.
