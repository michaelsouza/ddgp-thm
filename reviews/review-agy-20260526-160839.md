## 2. Framing Against the Abud "Impossible" Barrier

### 2.1 The Conceptual Gap
* **The Abud Barrier:** Abud et al. (2024) proved a fundamental global impossibility result: *the number of solutions of a general DDGP instance cannot be counted from the graph alone (a purely combinatorial method) in full generality.* They demonstrated this by showing that numerical edge weights can cause branch choices to fail the triangle inequality with a positive probability, meaning that two geometrically valid positions for a vertex may collapse into zero or one valid position depending entirely on the edge weights (numerical data) rather than the graph topology (combinatorial data).
* **Our Breakthrough:** Our paper presents the **constructive resolution** to this barrier. We bypass this global impossibility by shifting our focus from a global combinatorial graph-only model to a **local active subproblem under a generic mirror-separation assumption**. Under this genericity assumption, we prove that the solution count is indeed determined algebraically by a rank-nullity computation over $\mathbb{F}_2$.
* **Actionable Suggestion:** The authors should add a dedicated paragraph in the **Introduction** and **Section 9 (Scope and Genericity)** to explicitly frame our local rank-count theory as the constructive answer to the Abud impossibility barrier. By clearly stating that we overcome the weight-dependency limitation by assuming generic coordinate parameterization, we position our work as a major breakthrough directly succeeding the 2024 DAM paper.

## 3. Unifying Abud's "Combinatorial DDGP" as a Special Case

### 3.1 The Theoretical Unity
* **Abud's Positive Result:** In Section 3.2, Abud et al. (2024) define a special subclass called the **Combinatorial DDGP**, where *every* predecessor set $U_j$ induces a clique of size $K$ in $G$. They prove that for this subclass, combinatorial counting is possible and almost always yields exactly $2^{n-K}$ incongruent solutions (Corollary 3.6).
* **Our Unification:** This positive result is a natural **special case** of our general rank-count formula!
  - When every $U_j$ is a clique, any active base edge $\{a, b\} \in E_0[L_P]$ between predecessors of a vertex has both of its endpoints within the clique.
  - Consequently, if a generator support crosses the boundary, the fixed endpoint is guaranteed to lie on the mirror, so no active edges are violated.
  - Thus, the labeled violation matrix $V$ is identically the zero matrix ($V = 0$).
  - Substituting this into our rank formula:
    $$ \dim \mathcal{K}_F = \operatorname{rank}\begin{bmatrix}M\\ 0\end{bmatrix} - \operatorname{rank}(0) = \operatorname{rank}(M) - 0 = n - K $$
    Since $M$ is a triangular system of cone masks, it has full rank $n - K$.
    Therefore, the solution count is $|\Xi_F| = 2^{n-K}$, which perfectly and elegantly reproduces Abud's Corollary 3.6!
* **Actionable Suggestion:** Add a brief, elegant subsection in **Section 6 (Main Results)** or **Section 9 (Scope and Genericity)** demonstrating this unification. This shows that our theory is not a disjointed model but a powerful generalization that subsumes both the DMDGP symmetry counts and Abud's Combinatorial DDGP counts in a single unified algebraic framework.

## 4. Standardizing Terminology with Abud (2024)

### 4.1 Consistency in Definitions
* **The Terminology Gap:** Abud et al. (2024) establish a clean partition of the edge set $E$ into:
  1. **Discretization edges** ($E_D = \{\{i,j\} \in E \mid i \in U_j\}$), which are necessary to carry out the $K$-lateration steps.
  2. **Pruning edges** ($E_P = E \setminus E_D$), which represent the extra constraints used by the BP algorithm to check feasibility.
* **Our Current Draft:** Our draft uses terms like "local base edges $E_0[L_P]$" and "pruning set $P$". While correct in our local active context, it would be much cleaner to align with Abud's terminology.
* **Actionable Suggestion:** Update our definition of the active edge set $F$ in Section 2. Clearly state that we partition the local edge set into discretization edges $E_D$ and pruning edges $E_P$, and define the active edge set as $F = E_D[L_P] \cup E_P[L_P]$. This minor terminological alignment makes our paper immediately readable and familiar to anyone who has read the Abud paper.

## 5. Adding a Concrete Geometric Realization to the Worked Example

### 5.1 Moving Beyond Pure Combinatorics
* **The Abud Precedent:** Abud et al. (2024) use a highly effective concrete geometric realization in Example 3.3 (specifying physical coordinates and distances) to demonstrate how reflections behave and why the triangle inequality is violated depending on coordinates.
* **Our Current Draft:** Our worked example in **Section 8** is purely combinatorial (displaying only abstract predecessor sets and the resulting matrices $M$ and $V$).
* **Actionable Suggestion:** Recommend adding a **concrete geometric realization** (coordinates and distances) for our 6-vertex worked example. This will make the algebraic branch-shift propagation tangible and easy to follow.

We propose the following clean, equilateral/isosceles-based coordinates for the worked example ($K=2$ in $\mathbb{R}^2$):
1. **Initial Simplex ($V_0$):**
   Place the fixed seed vertices at:
   $$ v_1 = (0,0), \quad v_2 = (2,0), \quad v_3 = (0,2) $$
   This forms a clean right isosceles triangle.
2. **Predecessor and Coordinates for Non-seed Vertices:**
   * **Vertex $v_4$ ($U_4 = \{v_1, v_3\}$):** The predecessor mirror is the y-axis ($x=0$).
     - Let the reference position be $v_4 = (1, 1)$.
     - Its alternate branch choice (mirror reflection across the y-axis) is $v_4' = (-1, 1)$.
   * **Vertex $v_5$ ($U_5 = \{v_1, v_3\}$):** The predecessor mirror is also the y-axis ($x=0$).
     - Let the reference position be $v_5 = (2, 2)$.
     - Its alternate branch choice is $v_5' = (-2, 2)$.
   * **Vertex $v_6$ ($U_6 = \{v_1, v_4\}$):** The predecessor mirror is the line passing through $v_1 = (0,0)$ and $v_4$.
     - When $v_4$ is in its reference position $(1, 1)$, this mirror is the line $y = x$.
     - Let the reference position be $v_6 = (0, 2)$. Mirroring it across $y = x$ gives $v_6' = (2, 0)$.
3. **Dynamic Reflection Propagation:**
   Show what happens when we toggle the branch bit at $v_4$:
   - The position of $v_4$ mirrors to $v_4' = (-1, 1)$.
   - Because $v_6$ depends on $v_4$, its mirror *shifts dynamically* from the line $y = x$ to the line $y = -x$ (passing through $(0,0)$ and $(-1, 1)$).
   - Reflecting the reference position $v_6 = (0, 2)$ across this new mirror $y = -x$ yields $v_6'' = (-2, 0)$.
   - This beautifully illustrates the propagation of dependency cones: a branch choice at $v_4$ dynamically rotates the mirror of its descendant $v_6$, resulting in a completely different spatial position.
4. **Distance and Pruning Verification:**
   - Define the pruning edges $P = \{\{v_3, v_6\}, \{v_4, v_5\}\}$.
   - Let the reference distances be determined by the reference embedding:
     $$ d_{36} = \|v_3 - v_6\|_2 = \|(0,2) - (0,2)\|_2 = 0 $$ (or any non-zero distance if placed in general position, but simple coordinates are highly pedagogical).
     $$ d_{45} = \|v_4 - v_5\|_2 = \|(1,1) - (2,2)\|_2 = \sqrt{2} $$
   - Show how the simultaneous reflection $s \oplus \{v_4, v_5, v_6\}$ maps all coordinates to their mirrored counterparts across the y-axis, preserving all active distances and demonstrating the non-trivial feasible branch shift.

Adding this geometric section will elevate the paper from a purely abstract algebraic exercise to a deeply intuitive, visually checkable geometric proof.
