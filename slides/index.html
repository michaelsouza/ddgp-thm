Localization

Local subproblem

For a set of pruning edges \(P\), we keep the ancestors needed to fix the position of each endpoint. Here \(\operatorname{Fix}_U(u)\) is the ancestor closure of \(u\) in the discretization DAG needed to laterate it, and \(V_0\) are the seed vertices of the initial simplex.

\[ L_P=\bigcup_{\{u,v\}\in P} \left(\operatorname{Fix}_U(u)\cup\operatorname{Fix}_U(v)\right), \qquad B_P=L_P\setminus V_0 \]

Counted object

Local code

Given an active set \(F\subseteq E[L_P]\), typically \(F=E_0[L_P]\cup P\), \(\Xi_F\) is the set of local binary strings that realize every distance required by \(F\).

\[ \Xi_F = \{s\in\mathbb F_2^{B_P}\mid s \text{ realizes every } e\in F\} \]

The goal is to prove that \(\Xi_F\) is a translate of a vector space.

Generators

Labeled generator

A generator is not just a mask. It also carries the hyperplane that geometrically explains the reflection.

\[ g=(m_g,C_g), \qquad m_g\in\mathbb F_2^{B_P}, \qquad H_{C_g}=\operatorname{aff}(C_g) \]

Mask \(m_g\)

Binary operator on \(\mathbb F_2^{B_P}\). It flips the branch decisions in its support between 0 and 1.

Mirror clique \(C_g\)

Set of \(K\) predecessors that form the lateration simplex of some local vertex. Its affine space \(H_{C_g}=\operatorname{aff}(C_g)\) is the physical reflection plane.

Type 1

Cones

Changing the choice at \(q\) affects \(q\) and every local vertex that depends on it. The cone generator for \(q\) uses \(U_q\) as its mirror, the clique of \(K\) predecessors that laterate \(q\).

\[ g_q=(m_q,U_q), \qquad \operatorname{supp}(m_q)=B_P\cap\operatorname{Cone}_U(q) \]

Dependency cone

The set \(\operatorname{Cone}_U(q)\) contains \(q\) itself and every local vertex that depends on it in the discretization DAG.

Geometric effect

Flipping the binary decision at \(q\) rigidly reflects its whole dependency cone across the mirror \(U_q\).

These masks already generate all of \(\mathbb F_2^{B_P}\) (triangular matrix).

Type 2

Closures

Vertices generated by the same clique can be reflected as a single block with the same label.

\[ g_C=(m_C,C), \qquad \operatorname{supp}(m_C)= S_C= B_P\cap \bigcup_{w\in\operatorname{Gen}_P(C)} \operatorname{Cone}_U(w) \]

Closure \(S_C\)

Union of the dependency cones of the local vertices generated by clique \(C\), restricted to the local decision set \(B_P\).

Generators \(\operatorname{Gen}_P(C)\)

Set \(\{v_i \in L_P \mid U_i = C\}\) of all local vertices that share the same predecessor clique \(C\).

Redundant as masks, but not as labels.

Definition

Matrix \(M\)

Cone and closure generators produce a finite set of masks \(\{m_g\}\subset\mathbb F_2^{B_P}\). The matrix \(M\) gathers all of them as columns:

\[ M\colon\mathbb F_2^{\mathcal G}\to\mathbb F_2^{B_P}, \qquad M e_g = m_g \]

Image \(\operatorname{Im}(M)\)

The space of all masks reachable by linear combinations of generators. Since the cone generators form a triangular basis, \(M\) has rank \(|B_P|\).

Kernel \(\ker M\)

Combinations of generators whose resulting mask is zero. The closure generators are redundant as masks and generate \(\ker M\).

Geometry

Violation

We say that generator \(g\) violates edge \(e\in F\) when applying it changes the distance of \(e\). This occurs under two simultaneous conditions:

\[ g \text{ violates } e \;\Longleftrightarrow\; |e\cap S_g|=1 \;\text{and}\; e\setminus S_g\not\subseteq C_g \]

Crossing (\(|e\cap S_g|=1\))

The edge crosses the boundary of \(g\)'s support. One endpoint moves with the reflection and the other does not.

Outside the mirror (\(e\setminus S_g\not\subseteq C_g\))

The fixed endpoint is not in clique \(C_g\). Without anchoring in the mirror, the distance is not preserved.

This predicate becomes a labeled obstruction and then the vector \(\nu_F(g)\).

Crossing Compatibility Labeled violations

Algebra

Obstruction

When a violation occurs, it leaves an algebraic witness. This witness is indexed by the pair \((e,C)\): the observed edge and the mirror that explains the reflection.

\[ \mu_{e,C} = [C,a][C,b] \]

Geometric witness

The monomial \(\mu_{e,C}\) multiplies the oriented heights of the endpoints of \(e\) relative to mirror \(C\). It vanishes when either endpoint lies in the mirror.

Combinatorial label

The pair \((e,C)\) separates violations of the same edge caused by distinct mirrors. This is the information that the matrix \(V_F\) must preserve.

The labeled obstruction is the bridge between geometric reflection and linear algebra.

Obstruction monomial Oriented bracket Violation vector

Definition

Vector \(\nu_F(g)\)

Let \(\mathcal L_F=\{(e,C)\mid e\in F,\, C \text{ is the mirror clique of some generator}\}\). For each generator \(g\), define the violation vector \(\nu_F(g)\in\mathbb F_2^{\mathcal L_F}\). The double index is necessary: two generators with distinct mirrors over the same edge produce physically independent violations that should not cancel in \(\mathbb F_2\).

\[ \nu_F(g)_{(e,C)}=1 \;\Longleftrightarrow\; C=C_g \;\text{and}\; g \text{ violates } e \]

Indexing by \((e,C)\)

Separates violations by mirror. Two generators \(g,g'\) with \(C_g\neq C_{g'}\) that violate the same edge \(e\) occupy distinct coordinates; their effects do not cancel.

Matrix \(V_F\)

Stacking \(\nu_F(g)\) as columns for all generators gives the matrix \(V_F\). It measures which generator combinations leave violations active.

Quotient

Presentations

A single \(h\in\mathbb F_2^{B_P}\) admits multiple presentations \(x\) with \(Mx=h\). Two presentations differ by \(\ker M\), and their violation vectors differ by \(V_F(\ker M)\).

\[ R_F=V_F(\ker M), \qquad Q_F=\mathbb F_2^{\mathcal L_F}/R_F, \qquad \omega_F\colon h\mapsto[V_F(x)]\in Q_F \]

Pure presentations \(R_F\)

Generators in \(\ker M\) do not change the mask but can produce nonzero violation vectors. \(R_F\) captures this ambiguity and is quotiented out.

Canonical map \(\omega_F\)

In \(Q_F\), the class \([V_F(x)]\) is independent of the presentation chosen for \(h\). The next slide computes \(\mathcal K_F=\ker\omega_F\).

Stabilizer

Local symmetries

\(\mathcal K_F\) is the space of masks that admit some presentation with zero violation vector: the symmetries that preserve all active distances.

\[ \mathcal K_F=M(\ker V_F)=\ker\omega_F \]

\(M(\ker V_F)=\ker\omega_F\)

This follows from the definition of \(\omega_F\): if \([V_F(x)]=0\) in \(Q_F\), we can adjust \(x\) by a pure presentation in \(\ker M\) without changing \(h=Mx\), obtaining a presentation in \(\ker V_F\).

Particular solution \(s_0\)

For any \(s_0\in\Xi_F\) (a string that realizes the edges of \(F\)), the complete solution set is the translate \(\Xi_F=s_0\oplus\mathcal K_F\).

BP blocks

Admissible blocks

The sign cover is the set of all \(2^{|B_P|}\) binary assignments in \(\mathbb F_2^{B_P}\). An element \(h\in\mathcal K_F\) is zero-labeled (\(\omega_F(h)=0\)): added to any solution, it produces another solution.

Local base

The base edges \(E_0[L_P]\) lie inside \(F\). Thus each admissible block preserves the lateration structure while moving a region of the sign cover.

Composition

\(\mathcal K_F\) is closed under addition in \(\mathbb F_2\), so blocks compose. The sign cover splits into orbits of size \(|\mathcal K_F|=2^{\dim\mathcal K_F}\).

Theorem

Rank count

Since \(\Xi_F=s_0\oplus\mathcal K_F\), counting solutions means computing \(\dim\mathcal K_F\). By rank-nullity applied to the restriction of \(M\) to \(\ker V_F\):

\[ |\Xi_F|= 2^{\dim\mathcal K_F} = 2^{\operatorname{rank} \begin{bmatrix} M\\ V_F \end{bmatrix} - \operatorname{rank}(V_F)} \]

\(\operatorname{rank}(V_F)\) counts independent violation directions; \(\operatorname{rank}\bigl[{}^M_{V_F}\bigr]\) separates what is independent as a mask or as a violation. The difference is the space of independent masks with zero violation.

Rank-nullity Matrix \(M\) Matrix \(V_F\) Theorem

Linear identity

Stabilizer dimension

The dimension identity is purely linear. The map \(x\mapsto(Mx,V_Fx)\) stores, simultaneously, the mask produced by a presentation and the violations it leaves.

\[ \dim\mathcal K_F = \dim\ker V_F - \dim(\ker M\cap\ker V_F) \]

Expressing each term with ranks:

\[ \dim\mathcal K_F = \operatorname{rank} \begin{bmatrix} M\\ V_F \end{bmatrix} - \operatorname{rank}(V_F) \]

Why it works

Algebraic separation

For \(K\ge2\), each obstruction \((e,C)\) has a primitive algebraic signature that generically distinguishes it from the others. Writing \(\widehat x_v=(x_v^\top,1)\in\mathbb R^{K+1}\) for the homogeneous coordinates of \(v\):

\[ [C,z]= \det(\widehat x_{c_1},\dots,\widehat x_{c_K},\widehat x_z), \qquad \mu_{e,C}=[C,a][C,b] \]

Bracket \([C,z]\)

Oriented height of \(z\) relative to \(\operatorname{aff}(C)\). It is zero iff \(z\in\operatorname{aff}(C)\), i.e., iff \(z\) lies in the mirror.

Generic separation

Monomials of distinct obstructions \((e,C)\neq(e',C')\) are algebraically independent outside an algebraic exceptional set of measure zero.

Algorithm

Combinatorial computation

  1. Build \(L_P\), \(B_P\), and \(F=E_0[L_P]\cup P\).
  2. Assemble cone and base-clique generators.
  3. Identify labeled violations \((e,C)\).
  4. Compute ranks over \(\mathbb F_2\).

No exponential enumeration of branches is needed.

Limitation

Nongenericity

Accidental geometric coincidences can cancel residuals that are generically nonzero.

  • Special collinearities or coplanarities.
  • Endpoints accidentally landing on mirror hyperplanes.
  • Algebraic exceptional sets of measure zero.
